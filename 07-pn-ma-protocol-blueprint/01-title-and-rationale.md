## PART I: LEARNINGS & BEST PRACTICES

### 1.1 How to Write the Title

**What the examples teach us:**

Looking at the 6 PROSPERO records in this repo, titles fall into two patterns:

**Pattern A — PICO-in-title (most common for MAs):**
> "Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Botulinum Toxin Type A and Hyaluronic Acid Combination Therapy for Facial Rejuvenation: Assessing Clinical Efficacy, Patient Satisfaction, and Safety Outcomes" *(CRD42024559256)*

> "Efficacy and Outcomes of Collagen Biostimulator Injections for Facial Aesthetics: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis" *(CRD420251020585)*

> "A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Evaluating the Use of Exosomes in Aesthetics and Aesthetic Plastic Surgery" *(CRD420250655353)*

**Pattern B — Descriptive (for qualitative or non-MA):**
> "The effectiveness of polynucleotides in aesthetics medicine/ facial aesthetics : a systematic review" *(CRD42024588712)*
> *Note: Lampridou's title is notably vaguer — no "meta-analysis." This was honest; they only did narrative synthesis.*

**Key rule from PRISMA-P item 1a:** The title MUST identify the document as a protocol and preferably as a systematic review with or without meta-analysis. PROSPERO records that later publish as SRs without meta-analysis (like Lampridou) are fine — but if you INTEND a meta-analysis, say so.

**For your protocol:** You are doing a meta-analysis. Include "meta-analysis" in the title. Pattern A gives you the strongest signal. The existing Lampridou SR is narrative-only; your explicit "meta-analysis" in the title immediately differentiates you.

### 1.2 Writing the Rationale / Background

**What makes a rationale convincing?**

The 6 examples show three components that PROSPERO accepts as sufficient:

**Component 1 — Condition/Treatment context** (1–2 paragraphs)
> What is the treatment? Why does it matter? Who uses it? *(CRD42024559256, CRD420251152380)*

**Component 2 — Evidence-to-date summary** (1–2 paragraphs)
> What studies exist? What have prior SRs found? *(CRD42024588712, CRD420250655353)*

**Component 3 — The Gap** (1 paragraph — this is the most important)
> Why is another review needed now? Why not just rely on existing reviews? *(CRD420251152380: "Despite the growing evidence base, most studies have emphasized quantitative outcomes... patient-centered outcomes remain underexplored")*

**Common mistake #1:** Writing a mini-narrative review instead of a rationale. Keep it tight — 3–5 paragraphs total. PROSPERO is a registration, not a journal introduction.

**Common mistake #2:** Not explicitly naming the existing SRs and explaining why they're insufficient. This is especially critical in your case because TWO SRs already exist (Lampridou 2025, Kream 2026). If you don't convincingly argue why another is needed, an informed reader will ask "why aren't these enough?"

**Common mistake #3:** Claiming "no systematic review exists" when one does. This is factual inaccuracy and got the Lampridou record flagged with a version update. Be honest about what exists and precise about what's missing.

**The gap argument for your protocol:**

Your user already identified the key argument: *neither existing SR performed a quantitative meta-analysis.* This is a clean, defensible gap. But you need to be more specific:

- **Lampridou 2025:** Included only 9 studies, 219 patients, used broad inclusion (all PN uses — skin, hair, etc.), narrative only, no meta-analysis
- **Kream 2026:** Broader scope across all dermatologic PN/PDRN applications, not a focused efficacy analysis for skin rejuvenation
- **Your argument:** Since 2020, sufficient controlled trials (split-face RCTs and parallel-group RCTs) have accumulated using validated outcome measures (GAIS, Cutometer, VISIA) that can be pooled. The question is no longer "does PN work?" but "what is the pooled effect size across comparable outcomes?"

### 1.3 How Much Detail Is Enough?

From the examples, more detailed records (#1 BTX-A+HA, #5 Exosomes, #6 RFMN) tend to be more methodologically rigorous because they've thought through their choices. Sparse records (#3 Polynucleotides — surprisingly brief for a completed review) leave questions unanswered.

**Benchmark for adequate detail:**
- 3–5 paragraphs for rationale
- Each paragraph has a clear purpose (don't wander)
- Existing evidence cited with references (not just "some studies show")
- Gap is explicitly stated as a researchable question

### 1.4 PRISMA-P Requirements

PRISMA-P item 2 specifies:
> "Describe the rationale for the review in the context of what is already known."

The Explanation & Elaboration paper (Shamseer et al., BMJ 2015) emphasizes: **"The rationale should lead logically to a clearly stated question."** Don't just describe the treatment — build a case that ends with "and that's why we need this review."

## PART II: YOUR PROTOCOL DRAFT

### Draft Title

> **Polynucleotides for Skin Rejuvenation: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized and Split-Face Controlled Trials**

*Rationale for this title:* Identifies both SR and MA, names the intervention (polynucleotides) and outcome domain (skin rejuvenation), specifies study design (RCTs + split-face). Differentiates from Lampridou (narrative SR) and Kream (broad derm overview).

### Draft Rationale

> **Background:** Polynucleotide (PN) preparations — injectable formulations derived from salmon or human DNA fragments — have emerged as a widely used biostimulatory treatment for facial skin rejuvenation. Marketed for improving skin texture, elasticity, hydration, and wrinkle reduction, PN injections are commonly used in Asia and increasingly adopted in Europe and North America. The proposed mechanism involves fibroblast stimulation, increased collagen and elastin synthesis, and enhanced extracellular matrix hydration via nucleotide-mediated signalling pathways.
>
> **Existing evidence:** Two systematic reviews have recently synthesised the PN literature. Lampridou et al. (2025) included 9 studies (219 patients) across aesthetic PN applications and performed a narrative synthesis, concluding that evidence quality was low-to-moderate and precluded meta-analysis. Kream et al. (2026) provided a broader overview of PN and PDRN across all dermatologic applications (scarring, alopecia, wound healing, and rejuvenation) without performing a quantitative pooled analysis.
>
> **The gap:** Since the publication of these reviews, additional controlled trials have been published, and sufficient data have accumulated across multiple split-face and parallel-group RCTs using validated outcome measures (Global Aesthetic Improvement Scale, Cutometer elasticity measurements, VISIA imaging parameters). A quantitative synthesis of PN efficacy for skin rejuvenation — specifically, the pooled effect size across comparable outcomes — has not been performed. The present review aims to address this gap by conducting a meta-analysis of randomized and split-face controlled trials evaluating injectable polynucleotides for skin rejuvenation outcomes.

### 1.5 Lessons from the Lampridou 2025 PN Review (Your Direct Predecessor)

The existing PN SR by Lampridou et al. (*J Cosmet Dermatol* 2025, PMID: 39645667) is your closest comparator. Studying its PROSPERO record (#3 in this repo) and published paper reveals:

**What they did well:**
- Clear, focused PICO question
- PROSPERO-registered prospectively (CRD42024588712)
- Honest language restriction ("published in English")
- Narrative synthesis (did not over-promise MA they couldn't deliver)

**7 areas your protocol improves upon:**

| # | Lampridou 2025 | Your Protocol |
|---|----------------|---------------|
| 1 | CASP checklists (generic) | RoB 2 + ROBINS-I (domain-based) |
| 2 | No GRADE | GRADE pre-specified |
| 3 | No synthesis decision rule | "MA if ≥2 studies; otherwise SWiM narrative" |
| 4 | No outcome-time matrix | Pre-specified primary time point (3 months) + sensitivity time points |
| 5 | No split-face methodology | Explicit paired data handling with correlation imputation |
| 6 | No publication bias plan | Funnel plot, Egger's test, trim-and-fill, fail-safe N |
| 7 | No sensitivity analysis | Leave-one-out, RoB-stratified, fixed-effects comparison |
